The brief, as drawn

What the dice rolled

Ding, Dong!

a wifi doorbell + camera · device, cloud, and phone app

A wifi-connected doorbell and camera. Notify people on their phone when someone's at the door — even before the button is pressed. Tell a delivery driver to leave the parcel; keep a 24/7 record of anyone snooping. You design the device software and the web services that link it all back to the app.

Backlog (what it must do)

  • Ring loudly on button press
  • Notify the app the instant the button is pushed
  • Two-way audio + video once the ring is answered
  • Detect movement and notify before anyone rings
  • Record video + audio of a movement event
  • Let the app trigger a loud alarm
  • Onboard wifi credentials; change the password

The hardware you're handed

  • Wifi: host an open AP, or join a secure one
  • Video: stream to a web service
  • Audio: two-way stream to a web service
  • A button-press interrupt
  • Ring sounds baked into ROM
  • A needle-press reset button

Step 1 · before a line of design

Clarify, don't gap-fill

Evidence before output means naming what the brief doesn't say instead of quietly inventing it. Four questions decide most of the architecture — so they get asked first, not assumed.

What happens when the network is down?
The brief is silent. We assume the ring must still work offline — a doorbell that needs the cloud to chime is a broken doorbell. That single assumption reshapes the whole device design.
Who watches the 24/7 video?
"Stream video to a service to detect movement" reads as always-on upload. That's a cost, privacy, and bandwidth bomb. We flag it and design around it rather than build it literally.
How secure is the onboarding window?
The device hosts an open access point to receive wifi credentials. That's an unauthenticated door into someone's home network. Named up front as the sharpest edge.
How real-time is "two-way audio"?
A conversation needs sub-second latency; a recording doesn't. Treating them as one "media" feature would over- or under-build both. They're different problems.

Step 2 · the decisions that matter

Five calls, through the lenses

Not "best practices" — trade-offs, each named with the force it answers to. The lens in the corner is the tool that made the call.

Balanced coupling · reliability

The ring is local; the cloud is best-effort

Button interrupt → ROM sound, entirely on-device. Notify, stream, and record are all best-effort on top. The core function is severed from the network by design.

Trade-off: the doorbell always chimes, even when every service is down — the same "core survives its dependencies" rule we chaos-test our own services against.

Trade-off analysis · the Hard Parts

Split notifications from media

Notifications are tiny and high-fan-out; media is heavy and low-fan-out. They scale, fail, and cost differently, so they're separate services behind a contract, not one endpoint.

Trade-off: two services plus a contract, versus one bottleneck that a video stream can starve of notification capacity.

Architect elevator · cost / latency

Streams go peer-to-peer; the cloud only introduces them

Two-way audio can't afford a cloud round-trip. WebRTC connects app and device directly; the cloud handles signaling and a relay only as fallback.

Trade-off: NAT-traversal complexity, bought back in latency and bandwidth cost — and reversible: fall back to relay if P2P fails.

Well-Architected · cost, privacy, sustainability

Detect motion at the edge, not in the cloud

The device pre-filters motion and only uploads candidate events, not a 24/7 feed. The cloud confirms and records what matters.

Trade-off: squeezing inference onto constrained hardware, versus a bandwidth, storage, and privacy bill that grows with every camera-hour.

Well-Architected · security

Treat onboarding as the sharpest edge

The open-AP window is short-lived, gated by an out-of-band secret (a code on the device / the needle reset), and credentials are encrypted even over the open link.

Trade-off: a few seconds of onboarding friction, versus leaving an unauthenticated door into the customer's home network.

Step 4 · the part that actually ships software

Build → review → ship → learn

Everything above is a spec — it becomes a plan, and both open as living draft PRs before a line of firmware exists. The design is the easy half. This is the half people mean when they ask whether AI-driven work is any good: what happens between "here's the plan" and "it's running in the field." Same doorbell, traced through the loop.

Build · fan out, one contract

Three sessions, no collision

The seam between device, cloud, and app was frozen in the spec (Step 3), so three worktree-isolated sessions run in parallel against that one contract — nobody's waiting on anybody. Handoffs ride a focused Slack thread; the reason behind each contract decision lives in the PR comments, not in someone's head.

firmware · ring, wifi SM, edge filterservices · registry, notify, mediaapp · onboarding, live view

Review · the gate, no exceptions

Where the drift gets caught

Every diff clears the same gate — AI-written code gets more scrutiny, not a pass. On this draw the review catches a real one: the app lane shipped wifi credentials over the open AP before the out-of-band gate from Step 2 landed. That's decision-to-code drift — the plan said one thing, the diff did another — caught at the seam, not in the field.

Gate: Copilot first → a human graduates it → a second human merges · nobody merges their own work

Ship · reversible by construction

A backout path before the blast radius

Firmware rolls to a canary cohort behind a flag; services deploy dark, then flip. The P2P→relay fallback from Step 2 is proven in staging before a real network gets to break it. Nothing ships that we can't take back in one move — optimize for rollback, minimize blast radius.

Ship: flag-gated · canary cohort · one-move rollback

Learn · production talks back

The signal becomes doctrine

Datadog watches the fleet and the services the moment they ship. When carrier-grade NAT starves the P2P path in the wild — the failure a whiteboard never surfaces — that's not just a fix. The gotcha becomes a doctrine note; the onboarding-security step becomes a shared skill the next session inherits; the signal sharpens the next spec.
